Master Glossary A to Z


Section A

    Absolute file path: The full file path, which starts from the root. (Context: Essential for navigating Linux directories accurately.)

    Access controls: Security controls that manage access, authorization, and accountability of information. (Context: Primary mechanism for enforcing Confidentiality.)

    Active packet sniffing: An attack where data packets are manipulated in transit. (Context: Used to intercept/alter unencrypted traffic.)

    Address Resolution Protocol (ARP): A network protocol used to determine the MAC address of the next router or device. (Context: Frequently targeted by ARP spoofing.)

    Advanced persistent threat (APT): An instance when a threat actor maintains unauthorized access to a system for an extended period of time. (Context: Highly skilled attackers, often state-sponsored, targeting critical infrastructure or IP.)

    Adversarial artificial intelligence (AI): A technique that manipulates AI and machine learning technology to conduct attacks efficiently. (Context: Core focus of advanced Red Teaming.)

    Adware: Legitimate software that displays digital advertisements in applications. (Context: Can sometimes be used maliciously to track user behavior or serve as a backdoor.)

    Algorithm: A set of rules used to solve a problem. (Context: The mathematical foundation behind encryption protocols and AI models.)

    Analysis: The investigation and validation of alerts. (Context: The daily operational duty of a SOC analyst.)

    Angler phishing: A technique where attackers impersonate customer service representatives on social media. (Context: Exploits user frustration to steal account details.)

    Anomaly-based analysis: A detection method that identifies abnormal behavior. (Context: Catches zero-day attacks by comparing traffic to a known "normal" baseline.)

    Antivirus software: A software program used to prevent, detect, and eliminate malware and viruses. (Context: A standard endpoint control.)

    Application programming interface (API) token: A small block of encrypted code containing information about a user. (Context: Used for secure authentication between systems without sending raw credentials.)

Section B

    Botnet: A network of infected computers controlled remotely by a threat actor. (Context: Frequently used to launch massive Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks.)

    Brute force attack: A password attack that involves submitting many passwords or passphrases with the hope of eventually guessing correctly. (Context: Mitigated by implementing account lockout policies after a certain number of failed attempts.)

    Business Email Compromise (BEC): A threat actor sends an email message that seems to be from a known source to make a legitimate-looking request. (Context: Usually designed to obtain a financial advantage or redirect wire transfers.)

Section C

    Cloud security: The process of ensuring assets stored in the cloud are properly configured and access is limited to authorized users. (Context: A rapidly growing subfield as enterprises migrate away from physical data centers.)

    Compliance: The process of adhering to internal standards and external regulations. (Context: Essential to avoid massive fines and legal penalties, such as those imposed by the EU AI Act or GDPR.)

    Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT): Specialized groups formed to manage and respond to computer security emergencies. (Context: Initially created in response to the devastating Morris Worm in 1988.)

    Cryptography: The science of hiding information to protect its confidentiality and integrity. (Context: Relies on complex mathematical algorithms to encrypt and decrypt data.)

Section D

    Digital forensics: The practice of identifying, analyzing, and preserving criminal evidence within networks. (Context: Critical for post-incident investigations and legal proceedings.)

    Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS): An attack where multiple compromised systems are used to target a single system, causing a Denial of Service. (Context: Directly attacks the "Availability" pillar of the CIA Triad.)

Section E - F

    Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR): Integrated, layered breach detection tools that continuously monitor endpoint devices. (Context: A major upgrade from traditional antivirus, relying on behavioral analysis rather than just static signatures.)

    Firewall: A network security device that monitors and filters incoming and outgoing network traffic based on an organization's previously established security policies. (Context: Acts as the primary barrier between a trusted internal network and an untrusted external network.)

Section I

    Identity and Access Management (IAM): The discipline of managing digital identities and controlling access to resources. (Context: A core CISSP domain ensuring that only the right individuals can access the right tools at the right times.)

    Incident response: The established policies and procedures followed to appropriately respond to a security breach. (Context: Prevents panic during a crisis by providing a strict roadmap for containment and remediation.)

    Insider threat: A current or former employee, external vendor, or trusted partner who poses a security risk. (Context: Can be intentional sabotage or accidental, such as an employee clicking a malicious link.)

    Intrusion Detection System (IDS): A system used to monitor network activity and alerts for possible intrusions. (Context: A passive defense tool; it flags the threat but does not actively stop it like an IPS does.)

Section L - M

    Least privilege: A security concept in which users are granted the minimum level of access required to perform their job functions. (Context: Limits the potential damage if a user's account is compromised.)

    Malware: Malicious software designed to harm devices or networks. (Context: Includes sub-categories like viruses, worms, ransomware, and spyware.)

    Multi-factor authentication (MFA): A security mechanism requiring two or more pieces of evidence to authenticate a user. (Context: A foundational security control that heavily mitigates the risk of stolen passwords.)

Section P - R

    Phishing: The use of digital communications to trick people into revealing sensitive data or deploying malicious software. (Context: The most common vector for social engineering attacks.)

    Playbook: A list of how to go through a certain detection, and what the analyst needs to look at in order to investigate. (Context: Standardizes the incident response process.)

    Personally Identifiable Information (PII): Data that can identify an individual, such as full name or physical address. (Context: Forms the basis of asset security and data privacy regulations.)

    Ransomware: A malicious attack where threat actors encrypt an organization's data and demand payment to restore access. (Context: One of the most financially damaging threats to modern enterprises.)

Section S

    Security Information and Event Management (SIEM): Tools that collect and analyze log data across an organization. (Context: Essential for identifying complex, multi-stage attacks that span across different systems.)

    Social engineering: A manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables. (Context: Relies on psychological triggers like Authority, Scarcity, and Urgency.)

    Sensitive PII (SPII): A highly restricted subset of PII (e.g., Social Security Numbers, biometric data) that falls under stricter handling guidelines. (Context: Breaches of SPII cause the most severe harm to individuals and organizations.)

    Spyware: Malware used to gather and sell information (such as private emails or keystrokes) without user consent. (Context: Often runs completely silently in the background.)

Section V - W

    Virus: Malicious code written to interfere with computer operations and cause damage. (Context: Unlike worms, viruses require a user to initiate them, such as opening an infected attachment.)

    Vulnerability scanner: Software that automatically compares existing common vulnerabilities and exposures against the technologies on the network. (Context: Used proactively to find unpatched software before attackers do.)

    Watering hole attack: A social engineering attack where a threat actor compromises a website frequently visited by a specific target group. (Context: Highly effective against localized organizations or specific industries.)

    Web-based exploits: Malicious code or behavior used to take advantage of coding flaws in a web application. (Context: Exploits like Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) or SQL Injection.)

    Whaling: A highly targeted form of spear phishing aimed at high-ranking executives. (Context: Used to gain access to highly sensitive, restricted corporate data.)

    Wide Area Network (WAN): A network that spans a large geographic area like a city, state, or country. (Context: The internet itself is the largest WAN.)

    Wi-Fi Protected Access (WPA): A wireless security protocol for devices to connect to the internet securely. (Context: Replaced the heavily flawed WEP protocol.)

    Wildcard: A special character that can be substituted with any other character. (Context: Heavily used in Linux terminal commands and scripting to batch process files.)

    Wireshark: An open-source network protocol analyzer. (Context: The industry standard tool for deep packet inspection and network troubleshooting.)

    World-writable file: A file that can be altered by anyone in the world. (Context: A massive security risk in Linux environments; usually flagged during security audits.)

    Worm: Malware that can duplicate and spread itself across systems on its own. (Context: The Blaster and Morris worms are historical examples of how devastating these can be to networks.)

Section X - Z

    X-Y-Z approach: A resume/interview formula: X = accomplishment; Y = quantifiable result; Z = method or skills used. (Context: A crucial strategy for landing entry-level SOC roles.)

    YARA-L: A computer language used to create rules for searching through ingested log data. (Context: Specific to modern SIEM tools like Google Chronicle for threat hunting.)

    Zero-day: An exploit that was previously unknown to the software vendor or the public. (Context: Highly dangerous because no official patch exists when the attack occurs in the wild.)

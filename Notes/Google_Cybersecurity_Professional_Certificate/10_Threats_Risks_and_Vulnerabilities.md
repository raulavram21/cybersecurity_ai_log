Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities


Core Security Terminology

    Asset: An item perceived as having value to an organization. (Context: This includes physical office spaces and computers, as well as digital assets like customers' PII and intellectual property (patents, copyrights).)

    Threat: Any circumstance or event that can negatively impact assets. (Context: Threats actively seek to exploit system weaknesses. A common example is Social Engineering, a manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables.)

    Vulnerability: A weakness that can be exploited by a threat. (Context: Examples include outdated firewalls or software, weak passwords, or unprotected confidential data. Security teams have direct control over patching vulnerabilities.)

    Risk: Anything that can impact the confidentiality, integrity, or availability (CIA) of an asset. (Context: Think of risk as the likelihood of a threat occurring. Crucially, both a vulnerability and a threat must be present for there to be a risk. For example, a lack of backup protocols represents a major risk if a ransomware threat occurs.)

Asset Risk Classification

Organizations rate risks at low, medium, and high levels depending on potential threats and the value of the underlying asset.

    Low-Risk Asset: Information that would not harm the organization's reputation or ongoing operations, and would not cause financial damage if compromised. (Context: This usually includes already-public information, such as website content or published research data.)

    Medium-Risk Asset: Information that is not available to the public and may cause some damage to the organization's finances, reputation, or ongoing operations if exposed. (Context: Internal communications or standard operational documents that are restricted but not legally regulated.)

    High-Risk Asset: Any information protected by regulations or laws, which if compromised, would have a severe negative impact on an organization's finances, ongoing operations, or reputation. (Context: This includes highly targeted data like Sensitive PII (SPII), standard PII, or proprietary intellectual property.)

CISSP Security Domains


Domain 1: Security and Risk Management

    Security Posture: An organization's ability to manage its defense of critical assets and data and react to change. (Context: Evaluates the overall readiness and resilience of the security infrastructure.)

    Core Focus Areas: Defining security goals and objectives, risk mitigation, compliance, business continuity, and legal regulations.

    Risk Mitigation: Having the right procedures and rules in place to quickly reduce the impact of a risk like a breach. (Context: Acknowledges that breaches will happen; mitigation focuses on minimizing the damage when they do.)

    Business Continuity: An organization's ability to maintain everyday productivity by establishing risk disaster recovery plans. (Context: Ensures the organization can survive and keep operating during and after a major incident.)

Domain 2: Asset Security

    Focus: Securing digital and physical assets, including the storage, maintenance, retention, and destruction of data. (Context: Deals with the entire lifecycle of data and physical devices to prevent unauthorized access or leaks.)

Domain 3: Security Architecture and Engineering

    Focus: Optimizing data security by ensuring effective tools, systems, and processes are in place to protect an organization's assets and data. (Context: Involves the actual design and building of secure infrastructure.)

Domain 4: Communication and Network Security

    Focus: Managing and securing physical networks and wireless communications. (Context: Protects data in transit across local and wide area networks.)

Domain 5: Identity and Access Management (IAM)

    Focus: Keeping data secure by making sure users follow established policies to control and manage assets. (Context: Ensures only the right people have the right access to the right resources.)

    Four Main Components:

        Identification: Verifying who a user is (e.g., providing a username, access card, or biometric data).

        Authentication: The verification process to prove a person's identity (e.g., entering a password or PIN).

        Authorization: Occurs after identity is confirmed; relates to their level of access based on their role in the organization.

        Accountability: Monitoring and recording user actions (like login attempts) to prove systems and data are used properly.

Domain 6: Security Assessment and Testing

    Focus: Conducting security control testing, collecting and analyzing data, and conducting security audits to monitor for risks, threats, and vulnerabilities. (Context: Proactively validates that security controls are actually working as intended.)

    Use Case: Analysts use testing evaluations and assessment reports to improve existing controls or implement new ones.

Domain 7: Security Operations

    Focus: Conducting investigations and implementing preventative measures. (Context: The day-to-day work of monitoring, detecting, and responding to incidents.)

    Forensic Investigation: Once a threat is neutralized, the collection of digital and physical evidence begins to identify when, how, and why the breach occurred.

Domain 8: Software Development Security

    Focus: Integrating secure coding practices and guidelines to create secure applications and services. (Context: Shifts security "left" in the development process rather than bolting it on at the end.)

    Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC): An efficient process used by teams to quickly build software products and features.

    Security Integration:

        Design Phase: Perform secure design reviews.

        Development/Testing Phases: Conduct secure code reviews.

        Deployment/Implementation Phase: Execute penetration testing to ensure security is embedded at every step.

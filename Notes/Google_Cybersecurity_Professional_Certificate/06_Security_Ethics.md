Security Ethics


Ethical Principles

    Confidentiality: The ethical duty to keep proprietary or private information, such as PII (Personally Identifiable Information), safe and secure. (Context: This is a cornerstone of professional ethics; leaking PII is a direct violation of trust and organizational policy.)

    Privacy Protection: Safeguarding personal information from unauthorized use, access, or distribution. (Context: As a security analyst, you must adhere strictly to company policies, such as only accessing employee data via secure databases and never sharing it in unapproved formats.)

    Laws: Rules that are recognized by a community and enforced by a governing entity. (Context: Unlike internal policies, laws carry significant legal consequences; security professionals use these as a foundational baseline for ethical decision-making.)

Core Ethical Responsibilities

    Ethical Duty: When you encounter sensitive data during your work, you are ethically bound to keep it confidential and safe, regardless of personal temptation or pressure.

    Policy Adherence: Security professionals are responsible for following their company’s established policies and procedures. (Context: Even if you have the technical access to a database, the ethical and policy-driven constraint is that you do not access it unless required by your job function.)

Ethical Concerns and Laws: Counterattacks

    United States Standpoint: Deploying a counterattack on a threat actor is illegal and viewed as "vigilantism." It risks escalating attacks, causing further harm, and creating international incidents if the attacker is a state-sponsored entity. Only approved government or military personnel are authorized to perform counterattacks.

    International Standpoint (ICJ Guidelines): Counterattacks are permissible only if they meet strict criteria: they must target only the aggressor, function as a request to stop, be non-escalatory, and have reversible effects.

    Practical Reality: Organizations generally do not conduct counterattacks. The legal parameters are difficult to measure and control, and inexperience in this field almost always leads to worse outcomes for the defender.

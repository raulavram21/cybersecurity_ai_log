Threats in Cybersecurity


Social Engineering Dynamics

    Social Engineering: The practice of manipulating individuals into performing actions or leaking confidential corporate data.

        (Context: Threat actors target human cognitive biases rather than technical entry barriers. Everyone is vulnerable, from entry-level workers to C-suite executives. Enforcing organizational caution involves routine security awareness training that teaches teams how to analyze messages for spelling errors, control what parameters they share over public social media platforms, and suppress curiosity when deals appear too good to be true.)

    Baiting: A social engineering tactic that tempts targets with a physical or digital item perceived to hold value to compromise system security.

        (Context: A common variation is USB baiting, where an adversary intentionally drops malware-loaded flash drives in a target firm's parking lot, relying on an employee's curiosity to pick up and plug the device into an internal workstation network.)

    Quid Pro Quo: A subset of baiting where an attacker promises a service, financial reward, or support asset in explicit exchange for information or infrastructure access.

        (Context: An adversary might call staff members pretending to be a bank loan officer offering optimized card interest rates, stating they only need immediate account parameters and secondary details to process the deal.)

    Tailgating: A physical security exploitation where an unauthorized individual closely follows an authorized employee into a badge-restricted building sector.

        (Context: Also known as piggybacking, this bypasses access control mechanisms entirely, allowing threat actors inside data centers or hardware rooms.)

    Watering Hole Attack: A highly targeted attack vector where a threat actor compromises a specific public or third-party web interface frequently visited by an organization's target demographic.

        (Context: Instead of targeting an enterprise network directly, hackers infect associated outer spaces with hidden web exploits. A historic example is the Holy Water Attack (2020), which secretly infected various religious, charity, and voluntary service websites to capture targeted visitors.)

Phishing Vector Classifications

    Phishing: The exploitation of electronic and digital communications to manipulate individuals into revealing sensitive credentials or launching malicious applications.

        (Context: Attackers build operational efficiencies by deploying pre-packaged phishing kits containing three core components: malicious attachments holding malware payloads, credential-harvesting data collection forms, and fraudulent web hyperlinks.)

    Email Phishing: The most common variation of phishing, executed via email correspondence mimicking a trusted brand, partner, or authority figure.

        (Context: Organizations deploy inline intrusion prevention systems (IPS) and advanced mail routing security layers to monitor message traffic volumes and catch anomalies or signature block structures before they reach a user's inbox.)

    Smishing: The use of cellular short message services (SMS) or mobile chat utilities (e.g., iMessage, WhatsApp) to execute phishing loops or impersonate a known contact.

    Vishing: The exploitation of electronic voice communications, telephone lines, or interactive voice response (IVR) platforms to harvest user data or impersonate corporate agents.

    Spear Phishing: A targeted subset of email phishing optimized specifically for a defined department, team role, or single high-value user within an enterprise (e.g., targeting the specific accountants of a small firm).

    Whaling: A high-severity category of spear phishing purposefully customized to exploit high-ranking executives (C-suite, board directors) to gain control over critical administrative structures.

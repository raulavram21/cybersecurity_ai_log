Asset Security


Core Security Risk Elements

Risk: Anything that can impact the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an asset.

    (Context: Our primary focus as security practitioners is to preserve the confidentiality, integrity, and availability components of the CIA triad. Defensive strategies rely on identifying and analyzing the relationships between three basic elements: assets, threats, and vulnerabilities.)

Asset: An item perceived as having tangible or operational value to an organization.

    (Context: Businesses must protect a wide range of assets, including digital assets (customer data, financial records), information systems (networks, software), physical assets (facilities, equipment, personnel), and intangible assets (brand reputation, intellectual property).)

Threat: Any circumstance or event that can negatively impact organizational assets.

Vulnerability: A flaw or weakness within an asset that can be exploited by a threat actor.
Lifecycle Asset Management

Asset Management: The process of tracking organizational assets and managing the specific risks that affect them.

    (Context: Security plans cannot protect unrecorded items. Effective asset management begins with maintaining an up-to-date asset inventory—a comprehensive catalog of assets that need to be protected. Without this record, organizations run the risk of losing track of critical infrastructure, hardware dependencies, and sensitive data locations.)

Determining Asset Profile Factors:

    What assets you have

    Where those assets are located

    Who owns or is responsible for them

    How important or critical they are to business operations

Data & Asset Classification Schemes

Asset Classification: The practice of labeling assets based on their inherent sensitivity and importance to an organization and their integrity.

    (Context: Classification helps organizations prioritize security resource spending, optimize IT licensing costs, comply with legal regulations, and apply appropriate cryptographic controls based on explicit risk profiles.)

Standard Corporate Classification Levels:

    Restricted: The highest level of classification, reserved for highly sensitive or need-to-know information. Exposure would cause catastrophic damage. Examples include proprietary intellectual property, protected health information (PHI), or cardholder payment data (PCI).

    Confidential: Assets whose unauthorized disclosure would lead to a significant negative impact on an organization's finances, operations, or legal compliance. Access is limited to personnel assigned to specific projects.

    Internal-Only: Data available to all authorized employees and business partners for daily operations. It can be shared freely within the boundaries of the organization but must never be leaked or shared externally.

    Public: The lowest level of classification, containing information that carries no negative consequences for the organization if released into the open public domain (e.g., marketing brochures, official press releases).

Security Frameworks and Controls


Security Frameworks Definition

    Security Frameworks: Guidelines used for building plans to help mitigate risks and threats to data and privacy. They provide a structured approach to implementing a security lifecycle.

    Core Components:

        Identifying and documenting security goals: The starting point where organizations define what they need to protect and compliance needs (e.g., GDPR).

        Setting guidelines to achieve security goals: Developing the specific rules, policies, and standards that map how the organization will reach those goals.

        Implementing strong security processes: The active execution of security measures to enforce guidelines (e.g., workflows for user profile updates or deletions).

        Monitoring and communicating results: Ongoing observation of the internal network and reporting performance/security findings to management or regulatory compliance officers.

Security Design: The CIA Triad

    CIA Triad: A foundational model that helps inform how organizations consider risk when setting up systems and security policies.

    Confidentiality: Keeping data private and restricted to authorized users.

    Integrity: The assurance that data is correct, authentic, and reliable.

    Availability: Ensuring data is accessible to those who are authorized to access it.

    Asset: An item perceived as having value to an organization.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework

    NIST CSF: A voluntary framework developed by the U.S.-based National Institute of Standards and Technology consisting of standards, guidelines, and best practices to manage cybersecurity risk.

    NIST RMF: A related framework focused on the Risk Management process.

Compliance, Standards, and Regulatory Controls

    Compliance: The process of adhering to internal standards and external regulations.

    Cyber Threat Framework (CTF): A U.S. government-developed framework providing a common language for describing cyber threat activity. (Context: Helps cybersecurity professionals analyze and share threat information efficiently to improve responses to evolving attacker tactics.)

    FERC-NERC: Regulation for organizations managing the power grid. (Context: Obligates organizations to mitigate/report security incidents and adhere to Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards.)

    FedRAMP: U.S. federal program standardizing security assessment/monitoring for cloud services. (Context: Ensures consistency between government agencies and third-party cloud providers.)

    CIS Controls: A nonprofit-developed set of controls to safeguard systems/networks. (Context: Helps organizations build a defense plan and provides actionable steps for incident response.)

    GDPR: E.U. regulation protecting residents' data processing and privacy rights. (Context: Requires transparency about data usage and mandatory 72-hour breach notification.)

    PCI DSS: International security standard for credit card info. (Context: Goal is to ensure secure storage/processing to reduce global credit card fraud.)

    HIPAA: U.S. federal law protecting patients' Protected Health Information (PHI). (Context: Governed by Privacy, Security, and Breach notification rules; requires strict controls like MFA for medical records.)

    ISO/IEC 27001: An international framework outlining requirements for an Information Security Management System (ISMS). (Context: Enables organizations to manage the security of assets like financial info, intellectual property, and employee data using standardized best practices and controls.)

    SOC 1 & SOC 2: AICPA-developed reports assessing financial compliance and organizational risk. (Context: Focuses on user access policies across levels to prevent fraud.)

Security Controls

    Security Controls: Safeguards designed to reduce and mitigate specific security risks. (Context: While frameworks provide the structure, controls are the specific measures organizations use to lower risk. They typically fall into three main categories designed to prevent, detect, or correct security issues.)

        Physical Controls: Safeguards that protect physical environments. (Context: Examples include gates, fences, locks, security guards, CCTV, motion detectors, and access badges.)

        Technical Controls: Technology-based measures used to protect networks and data. (Context: Examples include Firewalls, Multi-factor Authentication (MFA), and Antivirus software.)

        Administrative Controls: Rules, policies, and processes that dictate human behavior and operational structures. (Context: Examples include Separation of duties, Authorization policies, and Asset classification.)

Foundations and Frameworks


Core Definitions & Job Roles

    Cybersecurity: Protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or criminal exploitation. (Context: It focuses heavily on "people," as human error and social engineering are major threat vectors.)

    Security Analyst: A professional focused on monitoring networks for breaches, developing defense strategies, and researching threats to prevent incidents. (Context: Primarily focused on daily operations and investigations.)

    Security Engineer: A professional who builds and improves the actual detection systems and infrastructure. (Context: More project-focused than an analyst.)

    Red Team vs. Blue Team: The Blue Team defends systems and protects users, while the Red Team offensively "pokes holes" in defenses to uncover vulnerabilities. (Context: Creates a continuous loop of finding and patching flaws before real threat actors exploit them.)

    Core Analyst Skills: * Transferable: Communication, problem-solving under urgency, time management, and a growth mindset.

        Technical: Programming (automating tasks), SIEM tools (analyzing aggregated logs), and IDS (Intrusion Detection Systems for monitoring network boundaries).

The CIA Triad

    Core Mechanics: The supreme security model guiding all defensive architectures: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

    Confidentiality: Keeping data private to authorized users. (Context: Enforced via strong encryption and Access Controls.)

    Integrity: Ensuring data is accurate, authentic, and untampered. (Context: Enforced via file hashing and digital signatures.)

    Availability: Ensuring systems and data are accessible when needed. (Context: Enforced via redundant servers and backups.)

The 8 CISSP Security Domains

    Domain 1 (Security and Risk Management): Focuses on governance, compliance, legal regulations, ethics, and risk management.

    Domain 2 (Asset Security): Covers the collection, classification, handling, and secure disposal of data and physical media.

    Domain 3 (Security Architecture and Engineering): Involves designing secure engineering processes, cryptography, and physical security parameters.

    Domain 4 (Communication and Network Security): Focuses on secure communication channels, network protocols, and transmission technologies.

    Domain 5 (Identity and Access Management - IAM): Covers user authentication, authorization models, and accountability loops.

    Domain 6 (Security Assessment and Testing): Focuses on evaluating security controls through scanners, penetration testing, and continuous audits.

    Domain 7 (Security Operations): Manages daily monitoring, logging, incident response, and forensic investigations.

    Domain 8 (Software Development Security): Applies security concepts throughout the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC).
